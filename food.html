Korean Food

Home | Travel & Destinations | Culture & Traditions | Food

Korean cuisine is full of bold flavors, colorful dishes, and unique dining traditions. From sizzling street food to royal feasts, food is at the heart of Korean culture.

Staple Dishes

Bibimbap:

A colorful rice bowl with vegetables, beef, fried egg, and spicy gochujang sauce.

Kimchi:

Fermented cabbage with chili and garlic, served at every meal. Kimjang, the traditional process of making kimchi for the year, is held each October.

Samgyeopsal:

Grilled pork belly slices served with lettuce leaves, garlic, and ssamjang (a thick spicy paste).

Bulgogi:

Thinly sliced marinated beef, grilled to perfection.

Street Food

Tteokbokki:

Spicy rice cakes in a sweet and spicy sauce, often with fish cakes and boiled eggs.

Hotteok:

Sweet pancakes filled with brown sugar, nuts, and cinnamon.

Kimbap:

Rice rolls wrapped in seaweed, filled with vegetables, egg, and sometimes meat or fish.

Odeng:

Fish cake skewers served in a warm broth, perfect for chilly days.

Common Side Dishes (Banchan)

  • Kimchi (김치): Fermented vegetables, usually napa cabbage or radish, seasoned with chili, garlic, and ginger.
  • Japchae (잡채): Stir-fried glass noodles with vegetables and beef, seasoned with soy sauce and sesame oil.
  • Kongnamul (콩나물): Seasoned soybean sprouts, lightly blanched and dressed with sesame oil, garlic, and salt.
  • Gamja Jorim (감자조림): Braised potatoes simmered in soy sauce and sugar.
  • Oi Muchim (오이무침): Spicy cucumber salad with chili, vinegar, and sesame seeds.
  • Sigeumchi Namul (시금치나물): Seasoned spinach with sesame oil and garlic.
  • Jeon (전):Korean savory pancakes, made with a light batter (typically just eggs) and pan-fried with vegetables.

Dining Etiquette

Sharing is Caring:

Korean meals are often shared family-style, with everyone taking food from common dishes.

Use of Chopsticks and Spoons:

Chopsticks are used for solid foods, while spoons are used for soups and rice. It’s customary to wait for the eldest to start eating before you begin.

Drinking Etiquette:

When drinking alcohol, it’s customary to pour drinks for others rather than yourself. Hold your glass with both hands when receiving a drink from someone older or of higher status.

Respectful Behavior:

Avoid sticking chopsticks upright in a bowl of rice, as it resembles a funeral ritual. Also, try not to point with your chopsticks or use them to gesture while talking.
